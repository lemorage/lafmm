# Three Atomic Forces of Price Movement

Every price change in a liquid market is driven by a shift in one or more of
three atomic forces. This is not a model — it is an accounting identity. Price
is the present value of expected cash flows discounted at some rate, adjusted
by the imbalance of buyers and sellers at that moment. Change any of those
three inputs, and the price must move.

## The Atoms

### E[CF] — Expected Cash Flows

The market's consensus estimate of all future cash the asset will generate.

**What shifts it:**
- Earnings reports (beat/miss, guidance revision)
- Revenue growth acceleration or deceleration
- Margin expansion or compression
- New product launches, market entries, or exits
- Competitive disruption (new entrant, substitute, regulation)
- Demand shifts (macro cycle, secular trend, one-time event)
- Management changes that alter execution expectations
- Contract wins/losses, order backlog changes
- Regulatory approval or denial (biotech, energy, fintech)
- Geopolitical events that alter addressable market

**How to identify in headlines:**
Earnings, revenue, guidance, outlook, forecast, demand, orders, backlog,
market share, competition, launch, approval, capex, margin, growth, decline,
restructuring, acquisition synergies, write-down.

### r — Discount Rate

The rate at which future cash flows are discounted to present value. Higher r
means lower price, all else equal.

**What shifts it:**
- Central bank rate decisions (Fed, ECB, BOJ, PBOC)
- Inflation data (CPI, PCE, PPI, breakevens)
- Employment data (NFP, unemployment, wages)
- Yield curve movements (steepening, flattening, inversion)
- Credit spread changes (investment grade, high yield)
- Sovereign risk premium shifts
- Currency moves that alter cross-border discount rates
- Quantitative tightening/easing announcements
- Forward guidance changes in central bank communication

**How to identify in headlines:**
Fed, rate, interest, inflation, CPI, PCE, yields, Treasury, bond, monetary
policy, tightening, easing, pivot, dot plot, FOMC, credit spread, risk
premium, basis points.

**Cross-sector diagnostic:** Rate shifts move all assets simultaneously. When
multiple unrelated sectors move together, the cause is likely r. This is the
most powerful tool for distinguishing macro-driven moves from stock-specific
ones.

### S/D — Supply and Demand for the Security Itself

The mechanical imbalance of buyers and sellers, independent of fundamental
value. A stock can be cheap and still fall if sellers overwhelm buyers.

**What shifts it:**
- Share buybacks and issuance
- Insider buying and selling (Form 4 filings)
- Institutional fund flows (13F filings, ETF inflows/outflows)
- Index additions and deletions (S&P 500, Russell reconstitution)
- Sector rotation (growth to value, US to international)
- Short interest changes, short squeezes
- Options expiration and gamma hedging effects
- Lock-up expirations (post-IPO)
- Margin calls and forced liquidation
- Tax-loss selling (December) and January effect

**How to identify in headlines:**
Buyback, repurchase, insider, fund flow, ETF, inflow, outflow, rotation,
short interest, short squeeze, index rebalance, offering, dilution, lockup,
margin call, capitulation, accumulation.

## Multi-Atom Events

Some events hit two or three atoms simultaneously. These are the most
important to surface because the tensions between atoms often contain the
real signal.

| Event | Atoms | Why it matters |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| Fed rate cut during recession | r + E[CF] | Lower discount rate but worsening earnings — forces cancel |
| Massive buyback funded by debt | S/D + r | Reduces float but increases leverage and risk premium |
| Earnings beat + insider selling | E[CF] + S/D | Fundamentals improve but insiders disagree |
| Tariff announcement | E[CF] + S/D | Alters revenue expectations and triggers sector rotation |
| Index rebalance + strong earnings | S/D + E[CF] | Mechanical buying meets fundamental confirmation |

When you encounter a multi-atom event, tag all affected atoms and note the
direction of each (bullish or bearish on that atom). Tensions between atoms
are more valuable to surface than agreements.

## Boundaries

This framework organizes evidence. It does not:
- Predict which atom will dominate
- Assign magnitude to any shift
- Tell you whether the market has already priced the news in
- Replace judgment — it structures evidence for the human to judge

Use it when classification adds clarity. Skip it when a plain-language
summary serves the user better.
